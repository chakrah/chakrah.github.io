About me

I'm a Ph.D. candidate and Research & Teaching Assistant in the Department of Astronomy at the University of Geneva, Switzerland. Before moving to Switzerland, I completed my Masters in Astronomy at Leiden University, Netherlands, in 2021, and my Bachelor in Physics at the Center for Basic Sciences, India, in 2019. My research focuses on developing novel techniques to mitigate the biases introduced by stellar activity in the characterisation of transiting exoplanet atmospheres. To this end, I work with both ground and space-based telescopes, and a brief overview of my projects is provided below:

CHEOPS

CHEOPS (CHaracterising ExOPlanet Satellite) is a space telescope dedicated to the study of transiting exoplanets and their host stars using ultra-high precision photometry. I primarily use this facility to probe the early stages of planetary evolution and formation, by focusing on young exoplanets with ages < 100 Myr. Naturally, young planets orbit young stars, and these stars tend to be magnetically active in nature. This introduces significant challenges in accurately characterising the properties of the planets, as stellar activity can alter the shape of the transit light curves.

EULER

During my Ph.D., I served as the operations manager for the EulerCam instrument on the 1.2 meter Swiss Leonard Euler telescope at La Silla, Chile. The instrument has played a pivotal role in numerous exoplanet discoveries over the past decade, and I was responsible for overseeing its day-to-day operations, ensuring optimal performance and coordinating science observations. One of my key contributions was the development of a data reduction pipeline to monitor the long-term photometric variability of the host star. This is especially tricky as the instrument is mounted on an alt-azimuth mount, which introduces field rotation and consequently pixel shifts. The pipeline has been successfully implemented and is now routinely used to monitor the stellar activity induced photometric variability of several planet-hosting stars.

Software Development

As an astronomer, I routinely write code to analyse and interpret data from various telescopes. My primary programming language is Python, and most of my work is openly available on GitHub. In particular, I'm the lead developer of the open-source package SAGE (Stellar Activity Grid for Exoplanets), a tool designed to model the effects of stellar activity on exoplanet transit light curves and transmission spectra.
